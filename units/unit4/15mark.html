Unit 4: Nanomaterials

15 Mark Questions

1. Explain the working principle of Scanning Tunneling Microscope (STM) in detail. Describe its construction, different modes of operation, and applications in nanotechnology.

Answer:

Working Principle:

STM operates on the principle of quantum tunneling. When a sharp conducting tip is brought very close to a conducting surface, electrons tunnel through the vacuum gap between them, creating a measurable current.

Construction:

  1. Scanning System
    • Piezoelectric scanner for precise movement
    • Sharp metal tip (usually tungsten or platinum-iridium)
    • Sample stage
  2. Control System
    • Feedback electronics
    • Current amplifier
    • Computer interface
  3. Vibration Isolation
    • Spring suspension
    • Magnetic damping

Modes of Operation:

  1. Constant Current Mode
    • Maintains constant tunneling current
    • Tip height varies with surface topography
    • Better for rough surfaces
  2. Constant Height Mode
    • Maintains constant tip height
    • Current varies with surface features
    • Faster scanning

Applications:

  • Atomic-scale surface imaging
  • Manipulation of individual atoms
  • Surface modification
  • Nanostructure fabrication
  • Electronic property mapping

2. Discuss the quantum confinement effects in nanomaterials. Explain how the electronic properties change with dimensionality (0D, 1D, and 2D) and their applications.

Answer:

Quantum Confinement Effects:

When the size of a material becomes comparable to the de Broglie wavelength of electrons, quantum mechanical effects become significant, leading to discrete energy levels.

Dimensionality Effects:

  1. Zero-Dimensional (0D) - Quantum Dots
    • Complete confinement in all directions
    • Discrete energy levels like atoms
    • Size-dependent optical properties
    • Applications: LEDs, solar cells, bioimaging
  2. One-Dimensional (1D) - Quantum Wires
    • Confinement in two directions
    • Continuous energy in one direction
    • Enhanced carrier mobility
    • Applications: Field-effect transistors, sensors
  3. Two-Dimensional (2D) - Quantum Wells
    • Confinement in one direction
    • Continuous energy in two directions
    • Enhanced optical properties
    • Applications: Lasers, photodetectors

Mathematical Treatment:

For a particle in a box of size L:

E = (n²h²)/(8mL²)

where n is the quantum number, h is Planck's constant, and m is the effective mass.

Applications in Technology:

  • Quantum computing
  • High-efficiency solar cells
  • Advanced display technologies
  • Biosensors and medical imaging

3. Explain the synthesis methods of carbon nanotubes (CNTs). Describe their structure, properties, and applications in various fields.

Answer:

Synthesis Methods:

  1. Arc Discharge Method
    • High current between graphite electrodes
    • Forms CNTs in the plasma
    • Produces multi-walled CNTs
  2. Laser Ablation
    • High-power laser vaporizes graphite target
    • Forms CNTs in the vapor
    • Good for single-walled CNTs
  3. Chemical Vapor Deposition (CVD)
    • Catalytic decomposition of hydrocarbons
    • Most scalable method
    • Controlled growth on substrates

Structure:

  • Rolled graphene sheets
  • Chirality determines properties
  • Single-walled (SWCNT) and multi-walled (MWCNT)
  • Diameter: 1-50 nm
  • Length: up to several centimeters

Properties:

  1. Mechanical
    • High tensile strength
    • Excellent flexibility
    • Low density
  2. Electrical
    • Metallic or semiconducting
    • High current density
    • Ballistic transport
  3. Thermal
    • High thermal conductivity
    • Stable at high temperatures

Applications:

  • Composite materials
  • Electronics and sensors
  • Energy storage
  • Biomedical devices
  • Field emission displays
