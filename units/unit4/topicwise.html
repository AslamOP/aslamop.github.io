Unit 4: Semiconductor Physics

Topic-wise Questions

1. Intrinsic and Extrinsic Semiconductors

a) Define intrinsic and extrinsic semiconductors. Provide examples.

Answer: Intrinsic semiconductors are pure semiconductors without any impurities, such as silicon and germanium. Extrinsic semiconductors are doped with impurities to enhance conductivity and are classified as:

  • n-type: Doped with pentavalent atoms (e.g., phosphorus).
  • p-type: Doped with trivalent atoms (e.g., boron).
Doping introduces free electrons or holes, improving conductivity.

b) Compare direct energy gap versus indirect energy gap semiconductors.

Answer: Direct energy gap semiconductors (e.g., GaAs) allow electrons to transition directly between the conduction and valence bands, emitting photons efficiently. Indirect energy gap semiconductors (e.g., Si) require a change in momentum, making them less efficient for light emission.

2. Band Gap and Carrier Concentration

a) Explain the concept of the band gap in semiconductors.

Answer: The band gap is the energy difference between the valence band and the conduction band in a semiconductor. Electrons must gain energy equal to the band gap to move from the valence band to the conduction band, enabling electrical conductivity. For example, silicon has a band gap of 1.1 eV.

b) Derive the expression for carrier concentration in intrinsic semiconductors.

Answer: The carrier concentration in intrinsic semiconductors is given by: ni = sqrt(NcNv) exp(-Eg/2kT), where:

  • Nc, Nv: Effective density of states in the conduction and valence bands.
  • Eg: Band gap energy.
  • k: Boltzmann constant.
  • T: Absolute temperature.
This equation shows that carrier concentration increases with temperature.

3. Hall Effect

a) What is the Hall effect? Derive the expression for Hall voltage.

Answer: The Hall effect occurs when a magnetic field is applied perpendicular to the current in a conductor or semiconductor, causing a voltage (Hall voltage) to develop across the material. The Hall voltage is given by: VH = (IB)/(qnd), where:

  • I: Current.
  • B: Magnetic field strength.
  • q: Charge of the carrier.
  • n: Carrier concentration.
  • d: Thickness of the material.
The Hall effect is used to determine carrier type (n or p) and carrier concentration.

b) The Hall coefficient of a doped silicon sample is 3.66 x 104 m3/C and its resistivity is 8.93 x 10-3 Ω·m. Calculate the density of charge carriers.

Answer: The density of charge carriers (n) is calculated using the formula: n = 1 / (RH · q), where:

  • RH: Hall coefficient = 3.66 x 104 m3/C
  • q: Charge of an electron = 1.6 x 10-19 C
Substituting the values, n = 1 / (3.66 x 104 · 1.6 x 10-19). Simplify to find the result.

4. Semiconductor Diode Laser

a) Describe the construction and working of a semiconductor diode laser.

Answer: A semiconductor diode laser consists of a p-n junction diode made of direct band gap materials like GaAs. When forward biased, electrons and holes recombine at the junction, emitting coherent light. Key components include:

  • Active region: Where recombination occurs.
  • Reflective surfaces: To form an optical cavity.
  • Power source: Provides the forward bias.
Applications include fiber-optic communication and barcode scanning.

5. Mobility and Conductivity

a) Define mobility and derive its relation with conductivity.

Answer: Mobility (μ) is the measure of how quickly charge carriers move through a material under an electric field. Conductivity (σ) is related to mobility by: σ = nqμ, where:

  • n: Carrier concentration.
  • q: Charge of the carrier.
  • μ: Mobility.
This equation shows that higher mobility and carrier concentration lead to better conductivity.
