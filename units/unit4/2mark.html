Unit 4: Nanomaterials

2 Mark Questions

1. What is quantum confinement?

Answer: Quantum confinement is the phenomenon where the motion of electrons is restricted in one or more dimensions, leading to discrete energy levels. It occurs when the size of a material becomes comparable to the de Broglie wavelength of electrons.

2. Define nanomaterials and give examples.

Answer: Nanomaterials are materials with at least one dimension in the range of 1-100 nanometers. Examples include quantum dots, carbon nanotubes, graphene, and metal nanoparticles.

3. What is the working principle of Scanning Tunneling Microscope (STM)?

Answer: STM works on the principle of quantum tunneling. When a sharp conducting tip is brought very close to a conducting surface, electrons tunnel through the vacuum gap between them, creating a measurable current that depends on the tip-surface distance.

4. What are the different types of carbon nanotubes?

Answer: Carbon nanotubes are classified as single-walled (SWCNT) and multi-walled (MWCNT). They can also be categorized based on their chirality as armchair, zigzag, or chiral nanotubes.

5. What is the difference between quantum dots and quantum wells?

Answer: Quantum dots are zero-dimensional structures with confinement in all three dimensions, while quantum wells are two-dimensional structures with confinement in only one dimension. This leads to different electronic and optical properties.

6. What is the significance of V-number in optical fibers?

Answer: The V-number determines the number of modes that can propagate in an optical fiber. For single-mode operation, V < 2.405, while for multimode operation, V > 2.405.

7. What are the advantages of nanomaterials?

Answer: Nanomaterials offer enhanced mechanical strength, unique optical properties, improved electrical conductivity, and increased surface area. They also show novel quantum effects and can be used in various applications from electronics to medicine.
