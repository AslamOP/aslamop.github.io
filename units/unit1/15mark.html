Unit 1: Quantum Mechanics

15 Mark Questions

1. Explain the concept of wave-particle duality in detail. Discuss the experimental evidence supporting both wave and particle nature of matter, including the Davisson-Germer experiment and the double-slit experiment.

Answer:

Wave-Particle Duality:

The principle of wave-particle duality states that all particles exhibit both wave-like and particle-like properties. This duality is a cornerstone of quantum mechanics and is supported by experimental evidence.

Experimental Evidence:

  1. Davisson-Germer Experiment:
    • Electrons scattered from a nickel crystal produced diffraction patterns, confirming their wave nature.
    • This experiment validated de Broglie's hypothesis, which proposed that particles have an associated wavelength \( λ = h/p \).
  2. Double-Slit Experiment:
    • When electrons or photons pass through two slits, they create an interference pattern, demonstrating wave behavior.
    • When observed, the interference pattern collapses, revealing particle-like behavior.

Mathematical Formulation:

de Broglie wavelength: \( λ = h/p \)

Wave function: \( ψ(x,t) = Ae^{i(kx-ωt)} \)

2. Derive the time-independent Schrödinger equation and solve it for a particle in a one-dimensional infinite potential well. Discuss the physical significance of the solutions and their applications.

Answer:

Derivation of Time-independent Schrödinger Equation:

Starting from the time-dependent Schrödinger equation:

iħ ∂ψ/∂t = Ĥψ

Using separation of variables: \( ψ(x,t) = φ(x)T(t) \)

We obtain: \( - (ħ²/2m) d²φ/dx² + V(x)φ = Eφ \)

Solution for Infinite Potential Well:

Boundary conditions: \( φ(0) = φ(L) = 0 \)

Wave function: \( φ_n(x) = \sqrt{2/L} \sin(nπx/L) \)

Energy levels: \( E_n = (n²h²)/(8mL²) \)

Physical Significance:

  • Quantized energy levels indicate discrete states for the particle.
  • Zero-point energy is the minimum energy the particle can have.
  • Probability density \( |φ_n(x)|² \) shows the likelihood of finding the particle at a given position.

Applications:

  • Quantum dots and nanostructures.
  • Semiconductor devices.
  • Design of nanoscale materials.

3. Explain the concept of quantum tunneling and its applications in modern technology. Include detailed discussion of the scanning tunneling microscope (STM) and its working principle.

Answer:

Quantum Tunneling:

Quantum tunneling is a phenomenon where particles pass through a potential barrier higher than their energy, due to their wave-like nature.

Mathematical Treatment:

Transmission probability: \( T ≈ e^{-2αL} \)

where \( α = \sqrt{2m(V-E)}/ħ \), \( L \) is the barrier width, and \( V \) is the barrier height.

Scanning Tunneling Microscope (STM):

  1. Working Principle:
    • Electron tunneling occurs between a sharp tip and the surface when brought very close together.
    • The tunneling current depends on the distance, allowing atomic-scale imaging.
  2. Components:
    • Sharp metallic tip.
    • Piezoelectric scanner for precise movement.
    • Feedback electronics to maintain constant current.
    • Computer for data processing and imaging.

Applications:

  • Atomic-scale imaging and manipulation.
  • Material surface analysis.
  • Nanotechnology and quantum computing research.
