Unit 2: Lasers & Fiber Optics

Topicwise Questions

1. Laser Principles and Population Inversion

a) What is population inversion? Why is it necessary for laser action?

Answer: Population inversion is the condition where more atoms are in an excited state than in the ground state. It is necessary for net stimulated emission and laser action. This ensures that the emitted photons are coherent and amplified, forming the basis of laser operation.

b) Explain different pumping methods used in lasers with examples.

Answer: Pumping methods include:

  • Optical Pumping: Uses light sources like flash lamps or other lasers to excite atoms (e.g., Ruby, Nd:YAG lasers).
  • Electrical Pumping: Employs electric discharge to excite atoms (e.g., He-Ne, CO₂ lasers).
  • Chemical Pumping: Utilizes chemical reactions to achieve population inversion (e.g., chemical lasers).
  • Direct Conversion: Involves direct electrical current in semiconductors (e.g., diode lasers).
These methods are chosen based on the type of laser and its application.

2. Types of Lasers

a) With neat diagrams, explain the construction and working of Nd:YAG, CO₂, and semiconductor lasers. Mention their applications.

Answer: Nd:YAG: A solid-state laser with Neodymium-doped Yttrium Aluminum Garnet as the active medium. It is optically pumped and used in surgery, material processing, and range finding. CO₂: A gas laser that operates on vibrational energy transitions, used in cutting, welding, and medical procedures. Semiconductor: A compact laser using a p-n junction, ideal for communication and consumer devices. Each laser type has unique advantages and applications.

3. Applications of Lasers

a) List and explain the major applications of lasers in industry, medicine, and research.

Answer: Industry: Lasers are used for precision cutting, welding, and marking. Medicine: They are employed in surgeries, dermatology, and ophthalmology. Research: Lasers enable advanced studies in spectroscopy, atomic physics, and quantum optics. Their high precision and control make them indispensable in these fields.

4. Fiber Optics: Structure, Types, and Communication

a) Explain the structure of an optical fiber and the principle of light propagation through it.

Answer: Optical fiber consists of a core, cladding, and protective coating. Light is guided through the core by total internal reflection at the core-cladding interface. This principle ensures efficient light transmission over long distances with minimal loss.

b) Classify optical fibers based on modes and refractive index profile.

Answer: Modes: Single-mode fibers support one propagation mode (V < 2.405), ideal for long-distance communication. Multimode fibers support multiple modes (V > 2.405), suitable for short distances. Refractive index: Step-index fibers have an abrupt change, while graded-index fibers have a gradual change, reducing modal dispersion.

c) Derive the expression for numerical aperture and explain its significance.

Answer: NA = sqrt(ncore2 - ncladding2). It determines the light-gathering ability and the number of modes in the fiber. A higher NA allows more light to enter, enhancing the fiber's efficiency.

5. Optical Fiber Communication and Comparison

a) Discuss the advantages and applications of optical fiber communication over conventional systems.

Answer: Optical fiber offers high bandwidth, low loss, immunity to EMI, secure data transmission, and is lightweight and flexible. Applications include telecommunications, internet, medical imaging, and sensors. These advantages make optical fibers superior to conventional systems in modern communication.
