Unit 2: Lasers & Fiber Optics

20 Mark Questions

1. Explain in detail the principle, construction, working, and applications of any two major lasers (Nd:YAG, CO₂, or semiconductor laser). Include energy level diagrams and discuss the advantages and limitations of each.

Answer:

Nd:YAG Laser:

  • Solid-state laser, Neodymium-doped Yttrium Aluminum Garnet as active medium.
  • Four-level system, optically pumped.
  • Energy level diagram: Shows ground, excited, and metastable states.
  • Applications: Material processing, medical surgery, range finding.
  • Advantages: High efficiency, good beam quality, pulsed/continuous operation.
  • Limitations: Requires optical pumping, cooling needed for high power.

CO₂ Laser:

  • Gas laser, CO₂ as active medium, electrically pumped.
  • Vibrational energy transitions, population inversion via N₂ energy transfer.
  • Energy level diagram: Shows vibrational levels of CO₂.
  • Applications: Cutting, welding, medical procedures.
  • Advantages: High power, efficient, long operational life.
  • Limitations: Bulky, requires gas handling.

Semiconductor Laser (if asked):

  • p-n junction as active region, direct electrical pumping.
  • Compact, efficient, used in communication and consumer devices.
  • Limitations: Sensitive to temperature, lower power than gas/solid-state lasers.

2. Derive the condition for population inversion and explain the different pumping schemes used in lasers. Discuss the importance of metastable states in laser operation.

Answer:

Population Inversion:

  • Condition: N₂ > N₁ (more atoms in excited state than ground state).
  • Essential for net stimulated emission and laser action.

Pumping Schemes:

  • Optical pumping (flash lamps, other lasers)
  • Electrical pumping (discharge, direct current)
  • Chemical pumping (exothermic reactions)
  • Direct conversion (semiconductor injection)

Metastable States:

  • Long-lived excited states allow accumulation of population.
  • Enable efficient population inversion and laser action.

3. Explain the structure, classification, and working of optical fibers. Derive the expression for numerical aperture and discuss its significance in communication systems.

Answer:

Structure:

  • Core, cladding, protective coating.
  • Total internal reflection guides light in the core.

Classification:

  • Step-index and graded-index fibers.
  • Single-mode and multimode fibers.

Numerical Aperture (NA):

  • NA = sqrt(ncore2 - ncladding2)
  • Determines light-gathering ability and number of modes.

Significance:

  • Higher NA: More light accepted, more modes (multimode fiber).
  • Lower NA: Single mode, less dispersion, better for long-distance communication.

4. Discuss the advantages and applications of lasers and optical fibers in modern technology. Compare optical fiber communication with conventional communication systems.

Answer:

Advantages of Lasers:

  • High intensity, coherence, monochromaticity, directionality.
  • Precision in cutting, welding, surgery, measurement.

Applications of Lasers:

  • Industry (cutting, welding, marking)
  • Medicine (surgery, dermatology, ophthalmology)
  • Communication (fiber optics, data transfer)
  • Research (spectroscopy, atomic physics)

Advantages of Optical Fibers:

  • High bandwidth, low loss, immunity to EMI, secure data transmission.
  • Lightweight, flexible, easy installation.

Applications of Optical Fibers:

  • Telecommunications, internet, medical imaging, sensors.

Comparison with Conventional Communication:

  • Optical fiber: Higher bandwidth, lower loss, more secure, less interference.
  • Conventional (copper): Lower bandwidth, higher loss, susceptible to EMI.
